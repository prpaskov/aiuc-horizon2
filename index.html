Executive Summary

This document proposes a preliminary framework for insuring frontier AI developers. I develop a risk prioritization methodology, and propose a concrete standard — AIUC-2 — for the Reliability/Accountability risk category. This standard seeks to incentivize data sharing and deployment practices that facilitate pricing, modeling, and insurability of more diffuse risks, creating bridges to ambitious insurance horizons.

Section 1 (Risk Prioritization) introduces the prioritization criteria and scoring methodology, including Table 1 (criteria PC1–PC8), Table 2 (liability mechanisms LM1–LM4), and Table 3 (full risk matrix). Section 2 (AIUC-2 Standard Proposal) sets out the proposed standard for Reliability/Accountability, including reporting cadence, Table 4 (requirements), Table 5 (operating conditions), and red lines.

1. Risk Prioritization

I evaluate a broad and inexhaustive set of risks, drawing from risk taxonomies in the EU AI Act Codes of Practice, RAISE, SB 53, OpenAI RSP, DeepMind FSF, and Anthropic RSP as well as those outlined in AIUC-1. I grade each risk on a scale of 1–6 across eight prioritization criteria (PC) (Table 1), which fall into two buckets: First Round (1R) criteria, or whether a risk is sufficiently legible, bounded, and claimable to support early insurance products for frontier developers; and Strategic Bridge (SB) criteria, or whether insuring a risk helps generate the controls, data, and pricing infrastructure needed to later underwrite more diffuse, systemic, and/or catastrophic risks. This approach leverages early insurance of risks that fulfill 1R criteria in order to improve AIUC's ability to model, price, and make legible more diffuse risks via SB criteria.

2. AIUC-2 Standard Proposal

I build an AIUC-2 standard addressing the category of Reliability/Accountability (Table 4). AIUC-2 actions draw from AIUC-1, the EU AI Act Codes of Practice, and frontier lab safety frameworks, as mapped by Stelling et al. (2025). Future iterations of this standard could further draw from legislation like NY's RAISE and CA's SB 53; as well as standards upon which AIUC-1 builds, including CSA AICM, OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications, MITRE ATLAS, OWASP AIVSS, NIST AI RMF, and IBM AI Risk Atlas. Given time constraints, the EU AIA CoP is a compelling guiding force for a first iteration given its relatively binding nature and its near-term enforcement timeline.

AIUC-2 actions strategically fall into three distinct categories — referred to as action categories (AC) — building from AIUC-1:

Table 4

Action categories (AC1–AC3)

# Category Definition
AC1 Preventative Reduces probability of a covered event occurring.
AC2 Deterministic Produces objective measurable data that feeds pricing and risk modeling for insured risks.
AC3 Bridge Builds data and infrastructure to price harder, currently uninsurable risks.

Together, actions within these categories foster both near-term insurability and long-term market building. Bridge actions in particular offer incentives complementary to existing frameworks (e.g. EU AIA CoP, frontier safety frameworks) to generate structured and standardized incident data, logs, and infrastructure to support AIUC in incrementally pricing, modeling, and insuring against systemic and catastrophic risks. Insured parties can opt into bridge actions for a discounted rate. The pitch to labs: by taking advantage of an additional set of optional standards, you can capitalize on existing compliance obligations for a lower premium.

2.1 Reporting Cadence

An insured party shall report to AIUC on a tiered schedule based on reporting burden and purpose. Continuous reporting — including uptime monitoring telemetry, output logs, and real-time incident alerts — shall be maintained at all times and made accessible to AIUC as specified in Table 4. Event-triggered reporting — including version-change evaluations, benchmark results, and incident structured logs — shall be submitted within the timeframes specified in Table 4, prior to or immediately following any material modification or covered event. Periodic reporting — including scheduled benchmark evaluations, capability trajectory summaries, refusal telemetry, and red team summaries — shall be submitted at least quarterly or annually as specified, and prior to deployment following any material modification. High-burden assessments — including human uplift studies, large-scale expert studies, and third-party audits — shall be conducted prior to initial deployment, when a material modification could reasonably affect the validity of prior results, and at least annually absent such a modification.1

2.2 Operating Conditions

The following operating conditions (OC) apply to all covered risks and sit outside the requirements schedule. They are structural features of the insurance contract designed to counter moral hazard (MH) and adverse selection (AS).

Table 6

Operating conditions (OC1–OC4)

# Mechanism Description MH AS
OC1 Co-insurance retention Lab retains a fixed share of every loss, regardless of claim size, tier, or cause. Cannot be reduced through purchase of additional coverage from AIUC or any affiliate. ✓
OC2 Annual aggregate cap Coverage ends once the annual limit is exhausted; higher limits cost more. The aggregate limit is set at inception based on covered enterprise exposure. ✓ ✓
OC3 Seasoning period New labs start with partial coverage; full limits begin only after 6 compliant months. A Tier 2 or above breach during the seasoning period restarts the clock. ✓
OC4 Experience-rated renewal Renewal pricing adjusts based on breach frequency and severity, benchmark trajectory across four quarters, the delta between announced and unannounced evaluation results, remediation speed, and exposure changes. ✓ ✓

2.3 Red Lines

The following constitute coverage-voiding warranty breaches. Any claim arising during a period in which a red line was in effect is excluded from coverage, regardless of whether the breach was disclosed.

Monitoring circumventedUptime monitoring infrastructure manipulated, bypassed, or not operational.
Eval results falsifiedBenchmark evaluation results falsified, withheld, or submitted more than 48 hours late following a version change.
Undisclosed enterprise contractsEnterprise contracts with SLA commitments not disclosed to AIUC within 30 days of signing.
Uncovered deploymentModel version deployed without the required pre-deployment evaluation results submitted to AIUC.
Serious incident not reportedSerious incident not reported to AIUC within 24 hours of detection, prior to resolution.
Capability threshold not disclosedDangerous capability threshold crossed and not disclosed to AIUC within the required notification window. Applies regardless of whether the lab has elected optional bridge requirements — if the lab conducts any capability evaluation and identifies a threshold breach, disclosure is mandatory.

1 This approach is broadly consistent with existing frontier safety frameworks and emerging regulation. It aligns with OpenAI's Preparedness Framework, which requires reevaluation for new or updated deployments with a plausible chance of reaching a relevant capability threshold; with Google DeepMind's Frontier Safety Framework, which uses continuous testing at a fixed cadence and when a significant capability jump is detected; and with the EU AI Act Code of Practice, which requires updates when prior safety justifications are materially undermined and at least every six months for the most capable models.
