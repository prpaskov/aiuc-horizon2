§2 Liability mechanisms as forcing functions

The four liability mechanisms in the table — contract, statute + private right of action, tort, and regulatory enforcement — differ on two dimensions that directly determine insurance tractability: whether compensation flows to the harmed party, and whether liability is pre-assigned or requires post-hoc proof.

Contract produces the cleanest triggers — liability is pre-assigned, payout is deterministic, and no causation proof is required beyond the contractual metric. Statute + private right of action predetermines the liability basis but still requires proof of violation, standing, and harm, making triggers stronger than tort but not as clean as contract. Tort assigns liability entirely post-hoc and requires proving duty, breach, causation, and damage — all contested for AI in most contexts, though specific sectors (medical, financial advice, product-integrated agentic systems) may become more tractable as doctrine develops. Regulatory enforcement predetermines liability but routes fines to the regulator rather than the victim — it cannot anchor a victim-compensation product, but can anchor defense cost cover, regulatory response costs, and compliance failure products.

The implication for standard design is that requirements which create contractual or statutory obligations — rather than leaving harmed parties to pursue tort — are the primary lever for converting currently uninsurable risks into insurable ones. The scores in this table reflect current legal conditions; scenarios where the mechanism column shows Contract or Statute+PRA are not necessarily already insurable, but they have the structural foundation that makes insurability achievable with the right standard design.

Contract Yes Pre-assigned Cleanest trigger. Payout metric contractually defined; no causation proof required. Scores 5–6 on trigger reliability.
Statute + private right of action Yes Pre-assigned Liability basis is predefined by law, but payout is not automatically clean. Proof of violation, standing, and harm (or statutory eligibility) still required. Scores 3–5 depending on how contested these elements are.
